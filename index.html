The Core Dilemma: Perils of Human-AI Collaboration

Despite AI's significant advantages, its integration also poses profound challenges to human cognition, team interaction, and decision quality.

Cognitive Debt

Over-reliance on external information search (the "Google Effect," Sparrow et al., 2011) and AI tools can lead to a decline in internal memory and critical thinking skills. In the long run, we may lose core competencies in independent analysis (Kosmyna et al., 2025).

Homogenization of Thought

When team members all turn to the same AI for answers, the standardized, optimized solutions it provides can converge the team's thinking, stifling necessary cognitive diversity and severely limiting the potential for breakthrough innovation (Page, 2007).

Algorithm Aversion

Humans have a psychological tendency to distrust or even reject decisions made by non-human entities (algorithms), especially after the algorithm makes even a minor mistake, this distrust is disproportionately amplified (Dietvorst, Simmons, & Massey, 2015).

Research Gap & Core Questions

While past research has focused on AI's technical functions, this study investigates the impact of its "social role."

  • Q1:How does framing AI as a "team member" versus a "personal assistant" affect team engagement?
  • Q2:Does treating AI as a team member foster more innovative team decisions?
  • Q3:Can this framing improve the speed and accuracy of team decision-making?
  • Q4:In AI-assisted decision-making, which is more effective: human critique or AI critique?

Standing on the Shoulders of Giants: Theoretical Foundations

This study builds its analytical framework by integrating classic theories from psychology, organizational behavior, and information systems.

Motivational Lens

Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000)

This theory posits that humans have three innate, basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When the external environment supports these needs, individuals exhibit the highest quality of intrinsic motivation and psychological well-being.

Cognitive Lens

Collective Intelligence Theory (Malone & Bernstein, 2015)

This theory argues that a group's collective intelligence is not a simple sum of its members' individual intelligence. It depends more on the quality of interaction among members, especially social perceptiveness, equality in conversational turn-taking, and the effective integration of cognitive diversity.

Process Lens

Socio-Technical Systems Theory (Trist, 1981)

This theory emphasizes that any organization is a composite of a "social system" (people, teams, culture) and a "technical system" (tools, equipment, processes). To achieve optimal performance, both subsystems must be jointly optimized, rather than focusing solely on technical efficiency.

Hypothesis 1: AI as a Collaborator Enhances Engagement

H1: When AI is treated as a team member rather than merely a tool, team members will feel more engaged and be more willing to express their own opinions, leading to higher participation.

Assistant Mode

Leads to responsibility offloading and passive reliance, causing members to feel detached and reducing motivation.

Collaborator Mode

AI as a conversational partner fulfills members' needs for autonomy and relatedness (Ryan & Deci, 2000), thereby boosting intrinsic motivation.

Hypothesis 2: AI as a Collaborator Sparks Innovation

H2: When AI is treated as a team member rather than merely a personal assistant, teams will generate more creative and original decisions, as members are more likely to contribute their own unique ideas.

Assistant Mode

Leads to "homogenization of thought" as members receive similar answers from the same AI, limiting cognitive diversity.

Collaborator Mode

Encourages members to think independently first, then collide their ideas with AI's, facilitating knowledge "externalization" and "combination" (Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995).

Hypothesis 3: AI as a Collaborator Optimizes Efficiency

H3: When AI is treated as a team member rather than merely a personal assistant, teams will be able to integrate ideas more effectively, leading to faster and more accurate decision-making.

Assistant Mode

Members discuss with information from AI that lacks a shared context, wasting time on alignment.

Collaborator Mode

AI acts as a real-time "information integrator," summarizing and clarifying to reduce the team's overall cognitive load (Sweller, 1988).

Core Contribution: AI Social Embeddedness Theory (AISET)

To explain the experimental findings, this study proposes a novel theoretical hypothesis, AISET, which integrates sociology, psychology, and human-computer interaction to effectively explain and guide the future development of AI.

The value of AI lies not only in its technical functions but, more importantly, in the degree to which it is perceived and accepted by the team as a "Socially Embedded Collaborator." This theory posits that AI should be designed as a social entity capable of interacting, adapting to context, and building connections with the team, rather than just being a passive tool. This provides a new framework that moves beyond "functional complementarity," emphasizing that AI's "social attributes" within a team are key to enhancing collaborative performance and overcoming algorithm aversion.

Theoretical Construction of AISET: Constructs and Mechanisms

AISET systematically explains how AI's social embeddedness drives team outcomes by integrating three foundational theories.

ü§ù

Interactive Engagement

The degree to which AI actively contributes and provides real-time feedback. High interactivity makes members feel supported and connected, triggering the Motivational Mechanism (SDT).

üéØ

Contextual Adaptability

AI's ability to adjust its behavior based on the specific task and team context. High adaptability allows AI to provide targeted insights, activating the Cognitive Mechanism (CI).

üîó

Social Connectedness

AI's ability to foster a sense of trust and belonging within the team. High connectedness allows AI to seamlessly integrate into the team's workflow, optimizing the Process Mechanism (STS).

Practical Implications: From Theory to Application

The findings of this study offer a clear roadmap for businesses and educational institutions.

üéìEducation

  • Use AI as a "brainstorming partner" to help students with divergent thinking.
  • Teachers must act as "critical guides," teaching students how to fact-check and critically evaluate AI outputs.
  • Design AI-assisted learning activities that transform AI into a scaffold for deeper learning, not a crutch that weakens thinking.

üè¢Business Management

  • When adopting AI, think beyond cost reduction and consider how to integrate it into collaborative workflows.
  • Frame AI in a "collaborator" role to unlock the organization's collective intelligence and innovative potential.
  • Redesign workflows and team communication patterns to establish best practices for human-AI teaming.

üé®Product Design

  • Future AI tools should move beyond simple Q&A interfaces to include a built-in "collaboration mode."
  • Provide adjustable levels of interactivity, proactivity, and transparency, allowing users to flexibly define AI's role.
  • Develop AI that can understand team context and dynamics to enable true human-AI co-creation.
